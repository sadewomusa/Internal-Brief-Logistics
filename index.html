Grade A Warehousing Park — Maleha / Sajaa
Planned Asset — Features & Anchor Strategy
In Planning▼
The park's location adjacent to the Etihad Rail station at Sajaa is a fundamental differentiator. No other industrial park in the Northern Emirates offers rail + port + bonded corridor access in one location. This must be the core pitch.
Grade A Physical Spec
  • Clear height: minimum 12m (ideally 14m for high-bay racking / ASRS compatibility)
  • Floor load: 5–7 tonnes/sqm (supports racking + forklift traffic)
  • Column spacing: 12m × 24m minimum (robotics-compatible floor layout)
  • ESFR sprinkler system (early suppression, fast response — required by 3PLs)
  • Dock door ratio: 1 per 800–1,000 sqm
  • FM2 floor flatness standard
  • LED lighting with motion sensors throughout
  • Solar panels on roof (UAE Net Zero 2050 + ESG tenant requirements)
  • EV charging in truck yard (10 units minimum to future-proof)
  • Fibre optic connectivity — 1Gbps minimum
  • RFID gantries at dock doors (auto-records every pallet in/out)
  • IoT sensors: temperature, humidity, movement — streamed to GT platform
  • CCTV with AI object detection and alerting
  • WMS pre-integrated — tenants plug SKUs, GT system manages inventory

🏢 Type A — Large BTS

10,000–50,000 sqm plots. Build-to-suit for 3PLs, multinationals. Long leases 5–15 years. Anchor tenant de-risks the park.

🏠 Type B — SME Units

500–3,000 sqm pre-built units. Short-term leases. Plug-and-play. Serves Northern Emirates manufacturers displaced from Dubai's full industrial zones.

Rail Confirmed▼

  • Rail ramp / container loading dock integrated into the dry port footprint

The Sharjah Corridor — Named Product
Non-Infrastructure Gap — Zero Capex Required
Needs Definition▼
GT has all the physical assets for a world-class bonded corridor — but it is not a named, priced, or marketed product. This is the lowest-cost, highest-return gap to close. A commercial product can be launched in 3–6 months with no capex.
What the Product Must Include
  • A defined name (e.g. "Sharjah Corridor", "GT Corridor", "SharjahLink" — TBD)
  • A defined transit time SLA: KCT discharge → Sajaa Dry Port in a guaranteed window
  • A defined price per TEU for each corridor leg (road + rail options)
  • A single contract covering: port → K-Flow → corridor → Sajaa → storage → rail → delivery
  • A single invoice — not 4 separate GT/Momentum invoices
  • A rate card aligned to Etihad Rail freight schedule windows
  • A dedicated commercial owner at Momentum Logistics who sells it

KCT Free Zone
The Single Biggest Missing Piece
Critical Gap▼
Every world-class container terminal of KCT's scale sits adjacent to a free zone. Jebel Ali has JAFZA. Khalifa Port has KEZAD. Fujairah Port has Fujairah Free Zone. KCT — handling 5M TEUs — has no adjacent free zone. This is the single layer that transforms KCT from a throughput terminal into an economic engine.
What a KCT Free Zone Unlocks
  • 100% foreign ownership for companies basing at KCT — not just shipping through it
  • 0% corporate tax on qualifying income
  • Permanent business licensing — companies locate here, not just handle cargo
  • Self-sustaining community of manufacturers, traders, maritime services, 3PLs
  • Same FDI magnet effect JAFZA had on Dubai — applied to Sharjah's east coast
  • Compete directly with Fujairah Free Zone for east coast business registration

Iran has struck Fujairah's oil terminals repeatedly throughout March 2026. Force majeure declared. Operations suspended multiple times. The Habshan–Fujairah pipeline — 1.5 million bpd bypassing Hormuz — has been a repeated target. KCT sits 70km away on the same coastline and has not been touched. This is not coincidence. Iran's targeting doctrine is specific: attack what bypasses Hormuz for strategic energy export. A commercial container terminal is not on that list. That distinction is KCT's protection — and the boundary that must not be crossed.

The Revenue Gap

Every vessel calling KCT burns fuel. None of that spend stays at KCT. The commercial case is straightforward — the customers are already here, the demand already exists, and the service is absent. The question is scope and structure.

VLSFO

Primary propulsion fuel for the container fleet post-IMO 2020. Fixed-roof storage. $440–$700/mt. The core product for any KCT fuel service — unremarkable infrastructure, standard at every commercial port.

MGO

Higher-margin distillate for port manoeuvring and ECA compliance. $700–$800/mt. Smaller volumes, better unit economics. Operationally standard.

LNG

Growing share of the container fleet — CMA CGM, MSC, and others are committed. Requires cryogenic infrastructure entirely separate from VLSFO/MGO. A distinct investment decision requiring actual fleet composition data at KCT before any commitment.

How GT Earns Without Becoming an Energy Company

Phase 1: Port Fee Model

Zero Capex. Immediate Revenue.

GT grants a licensed bunker trader port access. Trader brings barge and fuel. GT collects a per-tonne port service fee. No capital. No commodity exposure. No operational complexity. Market rate: $8–$15/tonne.

Start Here

Phase 2: Shore Storage

Own the Infrastructure. Charge for Access.

Small fixed-roof shore tanks sized strictly to documented Phase 1 demand. GT charges throughput fee to traders. GT is the landlord — not the trader, not the commodity holder. This is the Vopak model applied at KCT scale.

Phase 1 Data Must Justify This First
GT is the infrastructure owner. The bunker trader is the tenant. GT earns a fixed fee per tonne regardless of oil price. The trader carries commodity price risk, credit risk, and working capital. This structure does not change regardless of which phase or which option is pursued.
Special Consideration — The Geopolitical Dimension

Sharjah's political positioning is a genuine strategic asset — Sheikh Sultan's deliberate neutrality, distinct regional relationships, and distance from the Abraham Accords give KCT a threat profile that Dubai and Abu Dhabi cannot claim. This is not a formality. It is an active, maintained position that must inform every decision in this section. It is also not permanent — it is a function of leadership continuity and institutional discipline.

Infrastructure cannot be disguised. Floating roof tanks, VLCC berths, manifold piping configuration — a trained analyst identifies these on a site visit or from satellite imagery regardless of what any press release says. The physical signature of what is built determines the threat profile. Not the name. Not the license category. The structure itself.

🚨 Raises Alarm

  • Floating roof tanks — petroleum signature, identifiable from satellite
  • VLCC-capable berths — strategic crude export capability
  • Product tankers calling in bulk on a regular pattern
  • Any ADNOC or Abu Dhabi entity involvement
  • Any inland pipeline connection
  • Storage scale that exceeds container vessel demand math
  • Public narrative positioning KCT as Fujairah alternative or energy redundancy node
  • US or Israeli strategic capital in any KCT energy facility
  • Post-succession political realignment toward Abu Dhabi

✅ Does Not Raise Alarm

  • Fixed-roof tanks for VLSFO/MGO serving visiting container vessels
  • Bunker barge alongside a container ship during normal cargo operations
  • Depot scale proportional to documented vessel call demand
  • Private international operator — no state or strategic branding
  • No public energy narrative
  • Investment structured to recoup before political conditions could shift
  • Infrastructure indistinguishable from standard commercial port operations worldwide
The Core Question — How Far Does GT Go?

The conflict has opened a window. Fujairah is disrupted, vessels need alternatives, and KCT is operational. The question is whether GT uses this window opportunistically within a defined boundary — or pursues a larger energy ambition that changes what KCT is. Three positions.

Position A: Port Service Only

Contained. Executable. Safe.

Fuel as a commercial port amenity for visiting container vessels. Third-party operator. Fixed-roof tanks. No public narrative. Fast payback. No long-term energy optionality — but no long-term energy risk either.

Low Risk / Limited Ceiling

Position B: Disciplined Optionality

Start Small. Reserve the Future.

Begin with Phase 1 port service. Reserve substantially more land than Phase 1 requires. Design Phase 1 infrastructure with engineering specifications compatible with future expansion. Recoup Phase 1 before any Phase 2 commitment. The long-term optionality lives in engineering drawings and land reservation — never in external communications.

★ Recommended Position

Position C: Energy Terminal Ambition

High Ceiling. Irreversible Risk.

Scale toward a genuine east coast energy infrastructure play. Attracts oil traders, product tankers, scaled storage. Strong commercial ceiling — but visible, strategically legible, dependent on Sharjah's neutrality holding for 20+ years, and requires capabilities GT does not have. Once KCT is on an IRGC watchlist it is currently not on, it cannot be removed.

High Ceiling / Existential if Wrong
Position B is the only one that is simultaneously executable, commercially sound, and reversible. Position A leaves value on the table. Position C is a 20-year bet on political stability, leadership continuity, and GT's capability transformation — simultaneously. The downside of getting Position C wrong is not a bad investment. It is KCT.

The Constraints That Apply Regardless of Scenario

  • ✓No ADNOC or Abu Dhabi entity involvement — Sharjah's neutrality is a commercial asset, not a formality
